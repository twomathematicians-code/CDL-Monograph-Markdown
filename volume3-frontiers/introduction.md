# Introduction to Volume III
Volume III turns from the well-established core of deep learning to the open frontier. Each chapter takes a question that the field has not yet settled — what does a deep network's representation *mean*? why do larger models *scale*? when does an overparameterized network *generalize*? can a trained network be *understood mechanistically*? — and surveys the current state of the art, with attention to both what is known and what is contested. The volume closes with a chapter of explicit open problems, twenty of them, that we believe will shape the next decade of research.

The unifying perspective is that **deep learning is, in 2025, an empirical science in search of its theory**. The empirical record is rich and growing; the theory is partial and contested. This volume treats the principal candidates for that theory with intellectual honesty: where the theory is illuminating, we say so; where it is contested or vacuous, we say that too.

## Chapter map

**Chapter 11 — Representation Learning**. The geometry of the feature spaces learned by deep networks. Manifold hypotheses, linear representations, superposition, polysemantic neurons, the role of sparse coding.

**Chapter 12 — Scaling Laws**. The empirical regularity that loss decreases as a power law in compute, model size, and data; the theoretical explanations that have been proposed; the implications for compute-optimal training.

**Chapter 13 — Generalization Theory**. Why do overparameterized networks generalize? The classical theory (uniform convergence, Rademacher complexity) is vacuous; the modern candidates (implicit bias, PAC-Bayes, compression, NTK) are partial. We treat each with its strengths and limitations.

**Chapter 14 — Interpretability**. The program of reading networks from the inside. Circuits, superposition, sparse autoencoders, mechanistic interpretability, the "microscope" vs. "MRI" distinction.

**Chapter 15 — Open Problems**. Twenty problems, organized by area, with the current state of knowledge and the principal obstacles.

## How to read

The chapters of this volume are largely independent. Chapter 11 assumes Volume II; Chapter 12 assumes Volume I (optimization) and Volume II (architectures); Chapter 13 assumes Volume I (probability) and Volume II; Chapter 14 assumes Volume II and Chapter 11. Chapter 15 assumes everything.
